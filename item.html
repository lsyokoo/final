Fungal Light Orb


Description:

The Fungal Light Orb is a sustainable, bio-luminescent light source developed from genetically modified mycelium. Using bio-luminescent fungi (such as Armillaria mellea or engineered Pichia pastoris), these orbs provide eco-friendly lighting without electricity. They are perfect for illuminating dark underground spaces while requiring minimal maintenance.


How It Works:

The fungi are grown in a nutrient-rich agar base within a transparent, moisture-retaining shell.
Bio-luminescence is activated through a natural enzymatic reaction involving luciferase and luciferin, similar to the mechanism found in fireflies.
The orb maintains light output for 2-3 weeks before needing nutrient replenishment.


Tutorial Steps:

Step1.Prepare Materials:
-Sterile agar gel
-Mycelium spores (bio-luminescent strain)
-Transparent, sealed container (e.g., recycled plastic sphere)

Step2.Cultivate the Mycelium:
Spread spores onto the agar surface and incubate at 20-25°C in a dark, humid environment for 1-2 weeks.

Step3:Assemble the Orb:
Transfer the grown mycelium into the transparent container and seal it to retain moisture.

Step4:Activate Bio-luminescence:
Add a nutrient solution (mix with luciferin) to sustain light for 2-3 weeks.

Fungal Composite Furniture


Description:

Fungal Composite Furniture is a lightweight yet durable material created by growing fungal mycelium into molds. These composites are not only sustainable but also naturally fire-resistant and biodegradable. Commonly used species include Ganoderma lucidum (Reishi mushroom) and Pleurotus ostreatus (Oyster mushroom).

Applications:

Chairs, tables, and storage units.
Modular building panels for underground shelters.

Tutorial Steps:

Step1:Build a chair-shaped mold using wood, metal, or recycled plastic.
Step2:Prepare the Substrate: Mix agricultural waste (e.g., sawdust, straw) with fungal spores.
Step3:Fill the Mold: Pack the mold with the substrate and incubate in a humid environment at 25°C for 7-10 days.
Step4:Dry and Harden: Remove the mold and dry the composite in a low-heat oven (70°C) for 3-4 hours to kill spores and harden the structure.

Air Filtering Mask


Description:

This mask uses mycelium-based filters to purify underground air contaminated with harmful particles or toxic gases. The embedded fungi (e.g., Trametes versicolor or Pleurotus pulmonarius) metabolize certain volatile organic compounds (VOCs), enhancing air quality for users.

How It Works:

Mycelium layers are integrated with HEPA-like filter materials.
The fungal filter absorbs and breaks down pollutants, reducing their concentration.
Each mask is effective for up to 1 month with proper maintenance.

Tutorial Steps to Replace Filters:

1.Open the filter compartment of the mask.
2.Remove the used fungal filter and replace it with a fresh one.
3.Seal the compartment and ensure proper airflow before use.

Repurposed E-Waste Computer Terminals


Description:

These computer terminals are built using discarded laptop motherboards, outdated CPUs, and old CRT monitors. They serve as community hubs for accessing collective resources, learning modules, and communication networks.

Features:

Functionality: Basic operating systems for typing, data storage, and communication.
Connectivity: Networked with other terminals for information sharing.

Tutorial Steps:

1.Gather Parts: Salvage motherboards, processors, and RAM modules from old laptops.
2.Assemble the Computer: Install parts onto a recycled motherboard and connect to a functional power supply.
3.Choose an Operating System:Use lightweight Linux distributions optimized for low-resource environments.
4.Connect a Monitor: Attach a working CRT or LCD screen for display.
